Before You Begin: Seedlings & Germination

Before any plant can be planted into the NFT system, a tunnel or open ground, it must first be raised as a seedling. These first few weeks determine how strong and healthy the plant will be later — regardless of where it will eventually grow. The principles on this page apply to all three planting methods.

🧬
What Seedlings Need to Germinate
💧
Moisture The growing medium must be moist at all times, but never waterlogged. Mist lightly 1–2 times a day rather than soaking heavily — too much water displaces air and can cause the seed to rot.
🌡️
Warmth Most vegetable seeds (lettuce, spinach, mustard) germinate best between 18°C and 24°C. Too cold significantly delays germination; too warm can keep some seeds "dormant".
☀️
Light Before germination, most seeds don't need light — a dark, moist spot works well. As soon as the seed breaks through, place it immediately in bright light, but not direct sun. Too little light at this stage causes long, thin, weak seedlings (see Page 4).
🫧
Oxygen & Drainage Roots need oxygen just like leaves. A medium that stays wet all the time pushes air out of the pores and drowns the roots. Use a medium with good drainage (rockwool, perlite or coarse potting soil) and containers with drainage holes.
⏱️

Three Ways to Start

The same seedling principles (Page 2) apply everywhere — only the medium and container differ depending on where the seedling will eventually go.

1
NFT — Net Pot with Rockwool
Fill a 50 mm net pot ¾ full with rockwool (it holds moisture well for consistent germination), wet it, and press 1–2 seeds 0.5 cm deep. Perlite can be mixed in for extra drainage, but should not be used on its own — it dries out too quickly and doesn't hold enough moisture for germination. Once roots grow through the bottom of the net pot and are 2–3 cm white/cream, the seedling is ready to go straight into the NFT pipe's planting hole. See Chapter 3, Page 6 for the full step-by-step NFT seedling guide.
🧱 Photo/sketch: Rockwool cube with seed
🪴 Photo/sketch: Potting soil mixed with perlite (for tunnel seed trays)
2
Tunnel — Seed Trays with Potting Soil
Fill seed trays (or recycled containers with drainage holes) with fine potting soil or compost, with a little perlite mixed in for better drainage and aeration. Plant 1–2 seeds per cell, 0.5–1 cm deep, and keep moist in a sheltered, bright spot inside the tunnel. When seedlings have 2–3 true leaves (after ±2–3 weeks), transplant them directly into the tunnel bed.
⛺ Photo/sketch: Seed trays with seedlings in the tunnel
3
Open Ground — Direct Sowing or Seed Trays
In open ground you can either sow directly into the soil (works well when soil temperature and moisture are stable), or use seed trays as with the tunnel and transplant later. Seed trays give better control and higher germination success, especially early in the season. Seedlings going outside must first be hardened off — see Page 4.

Common Problems During Germination
🍄 Seedlings Fall Over and Rot at the Stem Damping-off (Fungal Disease)
Symptoms
  • Stem becomes thin and watery at soil level
  • Seedling suddenly falls over
  • Rotten smell from the medium
Cause
  • Too much water / poor drainage
  • Poor airflow around seedlings
  • Dirty containers or medium
Solution
  • Reduce watering — let the surface dry out slightly
  • Improve ventilation between trays
  • Use clean medium and containers; remove affected seedlings immediately
📏 Seedlings Become Long, Thin and Pale Etiolation (Too Little Light)
Symptoms
  • Long, thin stems
  • Leaves spaced far apart
  • Pale / light green colour
Cause
  • Too little light after germination
  • Light source too far from seedlings
  • Too warm without enough light
Solution
  • Move closer to a window or use grow lights 12–16 h/day
  • Lower temperature slightly
  • Turn trays daily for even growth
🌬️
Hardening Off — before Tunnel or Soil

Seedlings raised indoors or in a sheltered spot have soft leaves that aren't automatically resistant to direct sun, wind or temperature changes outside. Hardening off is the process of gradually exposing seedlings to outdoor conditions over 5–7 days, before moving them permanently to the tunnel or open ground. This step is not needed for NFT seedlings, since they remain within the same protected system environment.

DAY 1–2
1–2 hours in shade, outside out of the wind
DAY 3–4
2–4 hours, gentle morning sun
DAY 5–6
Half a day outside, more sun
DAY 7
Full day outside — ready for transplant

What is an NFT Hydroponic System?
💧 How does NFT work?

NFT stands for Nutrient Film Technique — a thin film of nutrient solution flowing over the roots.

Instead of soil, we use water that flows very slowly through tilted pipes — so slowly that it leaves only a thin "film" of water on the bottom of the pipe.

The plant roots hang inside these pipes. The lower tips of the roots touch the water film and absorb nutrients. The upper part of the roots stays in the air and breathes in oxygen.

🏫 What are our goals with this?
✅ Grow fresh vegetables at school — lettuce, spinach, strawberries and mustard
✅ Learn scientific skills — measurement, analysis, logbook
✅ Responsibility — every learner takes care of one side of the system
✅ Food security — understand how food is produced
✅ Entrepreneurship — vegetables can be sold to teachers and parents
⚖️
Advantages and Disadvantages of NFT
✅ Advantages
🌱 Faster growth — plants grow 30–50% faster than in soil
💧 Water-saving — up to 90% less water than traditional gardening
🧪 Precise control — you decide exactly which nutrients the plants get
🦟 Fewer pests — no soil = fewer soil-borne diseases
📏 Space-saving — A-frame design uses vertical space
🔄 Reusable water — water circulates back to the tank
⚠️ Disadvantages & Challenges
⚡ Power-dependent — the pump must run 24/7; power outages are dangerous
🌡 Water temperature — warm water holds less oxygen; roots can die
🧫 Disease spreads fast — one sick plant can infect the whole tank
💰 Setup cost — the initial cost is higher than an ordinary garden
📚 Knowledge required — EC, pH and oxygen must be measured regularly
🔧 Maintenance — tanks must be cleaned every 4–6 weeks
💡
Remember: The biggest danger in NFT is a pump failure. Without water circulation, roots die within 30 minutes. This is why we say in Chapter 5: the pump must be on 24/7.

The Three Core Measurements — What Does Each Measure & Why Do They Differ?
⚡
EC — Electrical Conductivity
Electrical Conductivity
Measures how well electricity moves through your water. More dissolved salts (nutrients) = higher conductivity. EC tells you the total strength of your nutrient solution — not which nutrients, just how much.
UnitmS/cm
NFT range1.0 – 2.2 mS/cm
Pure water EC≈ 0.0 mS/cm
💡 Think of EC as the "food concentration dial" — too low = hungry plants, too high = root burn damage.
🧪
pH — Acidity Level
Power of Hydrogen
Measures the concentration of hydrogen ions — i.e. how acidic or alkaline your water is. pH controls the chemical availability of nutrients. Even a perfect EC is useless if the pH blocks the nutrient the plant needs.
Scale0 (acidic) → 14 (alkaline)
NFT ideal5.5 – 6.8
Pure water pH7.0 (neutral)
💡 pH is the "key to the pantry" — the nutrients are present (EC is correct) but the wrong pH locks the door. The plant starves while food is all around it.
💧
TDS — Total Dissolved Solids
Total Dissolved Solids
Measures the same thing as EC — total dissolved salts — but expressed in ppm (parts per million) instead of mS/cm. Most TDS meters measure conductivity and multiply it by a conversion factor.
Unitppm (mg/L)
Conversion ×500EC 1.5 → 750 ppm
Conversion ×700EC 1.5 → 1050 ppm
💡 TDS and EC measure the same thing. Hydroponic growers use EC. Aquariums and drinking water use TDS. Know your meter's factor (×500 or ×700) — both are correct, just different scales.
⚠️
What Happens If Each Is Wrong?
⚡ EC Wrong
Too high: Burn damage — leaf edges turn brown, roots dry out, growth stops.

Too low: Plants starve — pale yellow colour, slow growth, poor yield.
🔴 Leaf tip burn
Too high — burn damage
🟡 Pale yellow leaves
Too low — starvation
Example: Lettuce EC of 3.2 mS/cm — leaf tips turn brown within 48 hours. Solution: dilute with fresh water to 1.5 mS/cm.
🧪 pH Wrong
Too high (>7.0): Iron, manganese and phosphate blocked — yellow leaves with green veins, purple stems.

Too low (<5.5): Calcium and magnesium blocked — leaf edges burn, growth stagnates.
🟡 Yellow leaves, green veins
Too high — blockage
🔴 Leaf edges burn
Too low — stagnation
Example: pH of 7.4 — lettuce leaves turn yellow even though EC is correct. Add pH-down dropwise until 6.2.
💧 TDS Wrong
TDS and EC have the same effect — TDS is just EC in a different unit.

Important: If your meter uses ×500 and another meter uses ×700, the readings will differ — both can be correct.
🔗
TDS symptoms = EC symptoms
Use the EC photos above as a reference
Example: Meter A reads 1050 ppm, Meter B reads 1470 ppm — same water, different factors (×500 vs ×700). Always use the same meter for consistent readings.

Oxygen in the NFT System — Why Is It So Critical?
🌿
Roots need oxygen just like leaves need CO₂ In an NFT system, the root hangs half in water, half in air — this is the cleverest part of NFT. The lower part of the root absorbs water and nutrients. The upper part hangs in the air space inside the pipe and absorbs oxygen directly. This oxygen drives cellular respiration, which enables nutrient uptake.

Too Little Oxygen — Causes
Root hypoxia → brown slime → plant death
Pump failure or power outage — still water loses oxygen within 2–4 hours. Roots begin to die off.
Water too warm (>26°C) — warm water holds less dissolved oxygen than cold water (Henry's Law).
Pipe slope too flat (<2°) — water pools instead of flowing and creates anaerobic dead zones.
Algae or bacterial outbreak — these organisms consume oxygen faster than it can dissolve, starving the roots.
Root volume blocks pipe — overgrown roots block the flow and eliminate the air space in the pipe.
📈
Too Much Oxygen — Is It Possible?
In NFT, more oxygen is almost always better
In practice — not dangerous for roots. Unlike soil, there is no upper limit where excess oxygen damages root tissue in hydroponics.
Air pump too strong in tank — strong bubbles make EC readings inaccurate. Measure after the bubbles settle.
Flow rate too aggressive — if water sprays rather than flows as a thin film, roots can dry out between cycles.
Signs of excellent oxygen — bright white shiny roots, no smell from the tank, fast growth, stable pH (no bacterial growth).
Rule of thumb: Keep water temperature below 22°C, maintain a 2–3% pipe slope, and let the pump run 24 hours a day.

ORP — Oxidation-Reduction Potential
🔋
What is ORP? ORP (Oxidation-Reduction Potential) measures, in millivolts (mV), how "clean" or "dirty" your nutrient solution is chemically. A high positive ORP means the water is oxygen-rich and harmful organisms such as Pythium (root rot) are suppressed. A low or negative ORP means the water is poor in oxygen — a sign that organic waste or bacterial growth is threatening the root zone.

Fertilizing — Own Products & the Dosing Template
🧪
What is "EC contribution"? Every fertilizer product raises the EC of your water in a fixed, predictable way — expressed as mS/cm per g/L (how much the EC rises if you dissolve 1 gram of the product in 1 litre of water). The Tank Monitor's Dosing Template uses this number to calculate exactly how many grams of each product you need to add to bring your EC to the target value.
1
Add your product in the Tank Monitor
Go to Dosing → Fertilizing and tap "+ Add Product". Fill in three fields: the Name (e.g. "Calcium Nitrate"), the EC Contribution (mS/cm per g/L — get it from the product label or calculate it yourself as explained below), and choose whether the product is Part A, Part B, or a single component.
☐ Name entered
☐ EC Contribution (mS/cm per g/L) entered
☐ Part A / Part B / Single component selected
2
Determine the EC contribution of a new product
No label value? Test it yourself: measure the EC of 1 litre of clean water (usually close to 0 mS/cm). Dissolve exactly 1 gram of the product in it and stir well until fully dissolved. Measure the EC again. The difference between the two readings is your EC contribution in mS/cm per g/L. For better accuracy, dissolve 5g in 5L and divide the EC increase by 5.
⚗️
Part A & Part B — Why Added Separately?
⚠️
Prevent a chemical reaction Concentrated fertilizer stock solutions often contain calcium (Part A) and phosphates/sulfates (Part B). If these two are mixed directly in concentrated form, they form an insoluble precipitate (a white, clumpy residue) that causes blockages and makes nutrients unavailable to the plant. Therefore always add Part A first to the water, stir well, and then slowly add Part B — never mix Part A and B directly with each other.
✅
The Dosing Template knows this rule — once you've registered products as "Part A" and "Part B", the recommendations will always show Part A first, followed by Part B, and lastly any single-component products.
🧮
How the Dosing Template Works
📐 Formula
grams to add = (target EC − current EC) × volume (L) ÷ EC contribution
📊
Example Your tank holds 100 L. Current EC = 1.2 mS/cm, target EC = 1.5 mS/cm (difference = 0.3 mS/cm). You've registered two products: Calcium Nitrate Omnica (Part A, EC contribution 0.75) and Hydroflow (Part B, EC contribution 0.60).

Calcium Nitrate Omnica: 0.3 × 100 ÷ 0.75 = 40 g — add first (Part A), stir well.
Hydroflow: 0.3 × 100 ÷ 0.60 = 50 g — add slowly afterward (Part B).

Place a submersible water pump in each tank. The pump sends water via a feed pipe to the top of Pipe 1. Slope is critical — don't skip this step Without the correct slope (2–3%), water will pool in the pipes instead of flowing. This causes oxygen deprivation in the roots and root rot within 48 hours. Use a spirit level to confirm the slope.
🫧
Incorrect flow rate causes oxygen deprivation in the roots In an NFT system, roots get their oxygen from the thin film of water and from the air space at the top of the pipe. If the flow is too slow (<1 L/min), the water sits in the pipe too long, the air space disappears and the roots suffocate — oxygen deprivation leads to root rot (Pythium) within 24–48 hours. If the flow is too fast (>2 L/min), the water sprays instead of flowing as a thin film, the roots become alternately wet and dry, and the air space is reduced. Measure regularly and keep the flow exactly between 1–2 L/min.

New Cycle — What Do You Do After a Harvest?

After every harvest the system must be cleaned and restarted before new seedlings are planted. This process usually takes 3–5 days and is essential to prevent disease.

DAY 1
Harvest & Remove
Harvest all plants. Remove all net pots and roots. Drain the tank completely.
DAY 1–2
Cleaning
Scrub pipes and tank. Rinse with clean water. Disinfect with hydrogen peroxide (3%).
DAY 2–3
Rinse & Dry
Rinse out all chemicals. Let pipes and tank dry completely. Check for damage.
DAY 3–4
Fresh Solution
Fill tank with fresh water. Mix new nutrients. Calibrate EC and pH. Circulate 24 hours.
DAY 4–5
Planting
Plant prepared seedlings. Check water doesn't touch growing medium. Monitor every 4 hours.

The 3 Rules You May Not Break

These rules protect your entire system. Breaking any one of them can destroy your crop within hours.

⚙️
Critical — Rule 1 of 3
The pump runs 24 hours a day, 7 days a week — no exceptions
In an NFT system, plant roots are always exposed to air inside the pipe. The only moisture they receive comes from the thin film of water that the pump pushes through the pipe. The moment the pump stops, that film disappears. Roots begin to dry out within minutes and die within 30 minutes — there is no recovery once roots are dead. Always keep a spare pump in stock. When a pump fails, you have 30 minutes to replace it.
💀
Without water flow: Roots dry out and die within 30 minutes. Dead roots cannot be revived. The entire plant must be replaced.
🧪
Critical — Rule 2 of 3
EC and pH must never be adjusted simultaneously — one change at a time
EC (nutrient strength) and pH (acidity) are both important, but must always be corrected one at a time. If you adjust both simultaneously, you won't know which change caused which effect. Always correct pH first, wait 30 minutes, measure again, and then adjust EC if still needed.
⚠️
Double correction risk: Over-correcting in both directions at once stresses plants far more than the original problem. Patience and one-step adjustments are essential.
💧
Critical — Rule 3 of 3
Never let the reservoir tank run dry
When the tank runs dry, the pump overheats. This creates a double failure: no pump AND no water. Tank levels drop faster on hot days due to evaporation and when plants are feeding heavily. Check all 4 tanks every morning without fail. If a tank runs low, top it up with a pre-mixed nutrient solution — not just clean water.
💀
Empty tank failure chain: Dry pump → burnt-out motor → no flow → dead roots. Two simultaneous failures that both need replacing.

What You Must Always Do

These 6 habits keep your system healthy, your water clean, and your crops growing.

📏
Setup
Maintain a 2–3% downward slope on all pipes
Pipes must slope slightly downward from inlet to outlet. This lets gravity pull the nutrient film along the pipe. Without a slope, water collects in the pipe, fully submerges roots and cuts off their oxygen — causing root rot.
📐Use a spirit level during installation. Recheck the slope after any pipe is moved or bumped.
⬜
Setup
Use only white or light-coloured pipes
Dark-coloured pipes absorb heat from sunlight and raise the water temperature inside. Water above 24°C holds less dissolved oxygen, meaning roots suffocate even if the pump is running. Light-coloured pipes also reduce internal algae growth.
🌡️Target water temperature: 18–22°C. Check it every morning with your EC meter if it has a temperature sensor.
🪣
Routine
Always keep the reservoir tank covered
An uncovered tank lets in sunlight, which causes algae growth. Algae consume the nutrients meant for your plants, clog the pump filter, and produce substances harmful to roots. A cover also reduces evaporation and prevents debris from falling in.
🌿Even a small gap lets in enough light for algae. Seal all openings around pipes that enter the tank lid.
🧹
Routine
Flush and clean tanks after every harvest — before a new crop
Always clean between crop cycles — this is the standard schedule. Drain the tank, scrub with diluted white vinegar, rinse thoroughly with clean water, and refill with fresh nutrient solution. Clean sooner if you see any of the following: slimy roots, brown deposits in pipes, bad odours, algae, pH/EC that won't stabilise, or slow flow. For strawberries and hot weather, biofilm and root problems build up faster — expect more frequent cleaning.
📅Never plant a new crop in an uncleaned system. The end of the cycle is the minimum — actual condition determines the real schedule.
🌡️
Routine
Measure water and air temperature every morning
Water temperature determines how much oxygen stays dissolved in the water — warmer water holds less oxygen and favours Pythium (root rot). Air temperature affects how fast plants grow and transpire. Act urgently if water rises above 24°C.
🎯Optimal: water 18–22°C · air 18–26°C. Use a digital water thermometer and a min/max air thermometer at the system.
📓
Important
Record EC and pH readings every morning
A single reading tells you the condition now. A written log tells you the trend — whether your system is stable, drifting, or deteriorating. Trends let you catch problems before plants show any visible symptoms, and save crops before damage occurs.
📈Use the monitoring log in this manual. Record date, EC, pH, water temperature and any observations per tank.

What You Must Never Do

These 6 mistakes are the most common causes of crop losses, pump problems, and root disease in NFT systems.

🌊
Critical
Roots must never be fully submerged in water
NFT stands for Nutrient Film Technique — the water is meant to be a thin film, not a pool. Roots must have open air above the film. Fully submerged roots have no access to oxygen and will develop root rot within 2–3 days, even if the water is clean.
💡If water collects in the pipe, inspect the system for blocked pipes or incorrect pipe slope.
🪵
Critical
Never let roots grow into and block the drain
Healthy roots grow vigorously and can fill the entire pipe, reach the drain opening and block it. Backed-up water floods the pipe and drowns all roots in it. Check drain openings weekly, especially in the 3rd and 4th week of a cycle when roots are largest.
🔍Check the drain end of each pipe weekly. Trim back roots that are approaching the opening.
⚗️
Important
Never mix Part A and Part B nutrients directly together
Most hydroponic nutrients come in two parts (A and B) that must be added to the tank separately. Mixing them together in concentrated form causes a chemical reaction that forms a white precipitate — the nutrients react and become permanently unavailable to plants.
✅Add Part A to the tank water first, stir well, then add Part B. Never pour them together undiluted.
💊
Important
Never add large doses of pH-Up or pH-Down at once
pH adjusters are highly concentrated. A single large dose can swing the pH 2–3 units past the target. Add a small amount — about 1ml per 20 litres — stir, wait 10–15 minutes, measure again before adding more.
⏱️pH takes time to stabilise in solution. Always wait 10–15 minutes after each addition before measuring again.
🍅
Setup
Don't grow heavy or large-rooted crops without a proper setup
Crops like tomatoes, cucumbers and bell peppers produce very large root masses that block water flow inside the pipe. Their heavy fruit also places physical strain on the pipes. Standard NFT pipes (50mm) are not suited to these crops.
✅Standard NFT is ideal for lettuce, spinach, herbs and strawberries — these are your best options for this system.
🍂
Routine
Never plant a new crop in an uncleaned system
Old root material left behind in pipes after harvest decays and feeds Pythium — the water mould that causes root rot. Pythium spores spread quickly through a shared water system. After every harvest, remove all root material, rinse pipes and disinfect with diluted 3% hydrogen peroxide.
🧼Disinfect: fill pipes with 3% hydrogen peroxide solution, let stand for 30 min, then rinse thoroughly with fresh water before planting.

Warning Signs — Act Immediately

If you see any of these signs, stop what you're doing and investigate immediately.

🟤
What you see
Brown, slimy or smelly roots
Cause: Pythium root rot. Drain the affected tank immediately. Remove all plants and root material. Scrub and disinfect the tank and pipes with diluted hydrogen peroxide. Check water temperature — Pythium thrives above 22°C. Only replant after complete disinfection.
🟡
What you see
EC is high but plants are yellow or weak
Cause: pH is wrong, not EC. When pH is out of range, plants cannot absorb nutrients even if EC is perfect — this is called nutrient lockout. Always correct pH first. Once pH is correct, measure EC again and adjust if still needed.
📉
What you see
EC drops faster than expected
Cause: Lush plant growth or rapid evaporation. First check the tank level — a low level affects readings. Top up with pre-mixed nutrient solution, not just clean water. Clean water lowers EC but doesn't replace nutrients.
🌡️
What you measure
Water temperature above 24°C
Cause: Water that's too warm holds little oxygen and promotes Pythium. Act urgently: move the system into shade, place an ice block in the tank as a temporary measure, and cover the tank lid completely. Avoid direct sunlight on pipes and tanks. Target 18–22°C — above 26°C the system is at risk.
🟢
What you see
Green coating on pipes, tank walls or inside pipes
Cause: Algae growth. Light is entering the system somewhere. Find and cover all openings. Clean affected surfaces with diluted chlorine solution (1:100), rinse thoroughly with clean water before reconnecting.

Plant Symptoms — Diagnose & Fix
💛 Yellow Old Leaves (Lower leaves yellow first) Nitrogen Deficiency
Symptoms
  • Old (lower) leaves yellow
  • New leaves stay green
  • Slow growth
  • Plant looks "light green"
Cause
  • EC too low — hungry plant
  • pH above 7.0 blocks N
  • Old nutrient solution
Solution
  • Increase EC with A+B nutrient
  • Check and correct pH
  • Replace 30% of solution
🟣 Purple / Red Colour on Leaf Stalks and Undersides Phosphate Deficiency
Symptoms
  • Purple/red stalks
  • Leaf undersides streaked purple
  • Growth slows
  • New leaves dark green
Cause
  • pH below 5.0 or above 7.0
  • Water temperature below 15°C
  • Phosphate low in nutrient
Solution
  • Adjust pH to 6.0–6.5
  • Warm water above 18°C
  • Check nutrient mixture
🟡 Yellow Leaves Between Veins (Veins stay green) Iron / Manganese Deficiency
Symptoms
  • New leaves yellow between veins
  • Veins themselves stay green
  • Starts at top leaves
Cause
  • pH above 6.5 blocks Fe & Mn
  • Root rot (no uptake)
  • Nutrient lacking chelated Fe
Solution
  • Lower pH to 5.8–6.2
  • Check roots
  • Use chelated iron supplement
🟤 Brown, Slimy Roots with Foul Smell Root Rot — Serious
Symptoms
  • Roots brown or black
  • Slimy to the touch
  • Foul smell from tank
  • Plant droops and wilts
Cause
  • Water too warm (>28°C)
  • No oxygen in water
  • Pythium or Fusarium fungus
  • Pump was off (water collected)
Solution
  • Remove all plants — even healthy-looking plants are latent carriers
  • Flush pipes with 3% hydrogen peroxide
  • Replace all solution
  • Lower water temperature
🔥 Leaf Edge Burn — Brown Edges on Leaves Calcium Deficiency / Heat
Symptoms
  • Brown, papery edges
  • Tipburn on inner leaves
  • Especially in lettuce during heat
Cause
  • Calcium not absorbed quickly enough
  • Too little airflow
  • Water temperature above 25°C
Solution
  • Improve air circulation (fan)
  • Lower water temperature
  • Place plants in shade during heat

Emergency Procedures
🔌 Power Outage > 2 hours
1
Immediately pour 5–10 L of water (with nutrient) through each pipe — roots begin to die after 30 min without flow
2
Repeat every 5–7 minutes — keep a filled bucket near the system so you can act quickly
3
Cover plants with shade cloth to reduce evaporation and keep root temperature low
4
Once power returns — turn pump on and measure EC/pH immediately; inspect roots for brown discolouration
🦠 Root Rot Outbreak
1
Remove all plants immediately — even seemingly healthy plants are latent carriers that will repeat the problem
2
Turn off pump, drain tank completely
3
Flush pipes with 3% hydrogen peroxide solution
4
Fill tank with fresh solution and start over
🌡 Heatwave (air temperature above 35°C)
1
Add ice blocks or cold water to the tank — keep water temperature below 24°C (optimal 18–22°C)
2
Place shade cloth over the system
3
Check that flow is not blocked — heat accelerates root growth
4
Monitor EC every 4 hours — heat raises EC quickly
❄️ Cold Snap (below 10°C)
1
Cover the system with plastic or shade cloth at night
2
Insulate the tank and pipes at night with an old blanket or plastic to retain heat
3
Increase EC slightly (10–15%) — cold requires more nutrients
4
Strawberries may grow better — lettuce slows down

Nutrient Imbalances — Magnesium & Potassium

Magnesium (Mg) and Potassium (K) are easily confused with other deficiencies because they also cause yellow or brown discolouration. The key to telling them apart is where on the plant the symptoms first appear.

🟢 Yellow Between Veins on Older Leaves (Veins stay green, "fishbone" pattern) Magnesium Deficiency
Symptoms
  • Yellow patches between veins on older/lower leaves
  • Veins themselves stay dark green
  • Sometimes purple/red tints on leaf edges
  • Spreads from bottom to top if it continues
Cause
  • Mg missing or too low in nutrient mix
  • Too much Potassium or Calcium displaces Mg uptake
  • EC too high — general ion competition
Solution
  • Add Epsom salt (Magnesium sulfate) — small amounts
  • Make sure EC doesn't run too high
  • Replace part of the nutrient solution
🌿 Sketch: fishbone pattern on old leaf
🟠 Brown/Scorched Leaf Edges on Older Leaves, Poor Fruit Potassium Deficiency
Symptoms
  • Brown, "burnt" edges on older leaves
  • Leaves sometimes curl downward
  • In strawberries: small/soft fruit, poor flavour
  • Plant falls over easily in wind (weak stems)
Cause
  • K concentration in nutrient too low
  • High Calcium/Magnesium displaces K uptake
  • Old nutrient solution — K is quickly depleted by the plant
Solution
  • Replace nutrient solution with fresh mix
  • Check fertiliser recipe — K source present?
  • In strawberries: increase K ratio during fruit set
🍓 Sketch: scorched leaf edge on old leaf
🔍
Distinguishing Mg/K from N Deficiency Nitrogen, Magnesium and Potassium deficiencies all start on the older (lower) leaves — look at the pattern: N deficiency = leaves turn evenly light green/yellow; Mg deficiency = yellow between the veins with green veins; K deficiency = scorched edges rather than overall discolouration.

Glossary — Technical Terms Explained
Term Reference Explanation
NFT
Abbr.		 Nutrient Film Technique Nutrient film technique. A method where a thin stream of nutrient-rich water flows through tilted pipes. Roots hang in this film and absorb nutrients while the upper part gets air.
EC
Abbr.		 Electrical Conductivity Electrical conductivity. Measures how many dissolved minerals (nutrients) are in the water. Measured in mS/cm. The higher the EC, the more nutrients — but too high burns roots.
pH
Abbr.		 Potential of Hydrogen Scale of 0–14 that measures how acidic or alkaline the water is. 7 = neutral. Below 7 = acidic. Above 7 = alkaline. The pH determines whether plants can absorb nutrients — the wrong pH "locks out" nutrients.
TDS
Abbr.		 Total Dissolved Solids Total dissolved solids in water. Measured in ppm (parts per million). TDS and EC measure the same thing — different units. Hydroponic growers usually use EC.
ppm
Abbr.		 Parts per Million Parts per million. Unit for TDS measurements. Means how many milligrams of solids are dissolved in one litre of water. 1 mS/cm EC ≈ 500–700 ppm TDS (depending on meter factor).
mS/cm
Unit		 Millisiemens/centimetre The standard unit for EC measurements in hydroponics. Typical ranges: lettuce 1.2–1.8 mS/cm, strawberries 1.0–1.4 mS/cm.
Pythium
Pythium spp.		 Root rot fungus Root rot fungus. The most dangerous disease organism in NFT systems. Grows quickly in warm water (above 22°C). Signs: brown, slimy roots and wilting plants. Spreads quickly through the whole tank.
Stolon
Runner / Stolon		 Vegetative propagule Long thin stem sent out by a mother strawberry plant. A new plantlet forms at the tip. Used to propagate strawberries vegetatively (without seed).
Bolting
Afrikaans: Saadskiet		 Premature flowering When a plant unexpectedly flowers and goes to seed due to heat or long day length. Lettuce, spinach and mustard spinach become bitter and inedible when they bolt. Avoid by planting in the right season.
Transplant Shock
Afrikaans: Oorplantings-kok		 Transplant stress Temporary stress a plant experiences after being moved. Signs: drooping leaves, slow growth for 24–72 hours. Normal — the plant recovers once roots can reach water.
Tipburn
Afrikaans: Tipbrand		 Calcium deficiency Brown, dried-out leaf edges on young leaves. Caused by calcium deficiency or poor water flow to new leaves. Avoid by maintaining flow and keeping EC correct.
Nutrient Lockout
Afrikaans: Voedingstof-blokkasie		 pH-induced deficiency When nutrients are present in the water (EC is correct) but the pH prevents the plant from absorbing them. Looks like a nutrient deficiency but the problem is the pH.
Propagation
Afrikaans: Propagasie		 Seedling production The process of growing seedlings — from seed or stolon — in rockwool, until the roots are long enough to be planted into the NFT system.
Rockwool
Afrikaans: Klipwol		 Inert growing medium Growing medium made from spun rock fibres. Holds water but also allows air through. Ideal for seedling propagation. pH-neutral and sterile. Reusable after disinfecting.
Perlite
Afrikaans: Perliet		 Volcanic glass medium Expanded volcanic glass. Light, porous white granules that balance air and water. Good alternative to rockwool. Chemically inert — does not affect EC or pH.
Root Rot
Afrikaans: Wortelvrot		 Root disease Decay of plant roots due to disease organisms (usually Pythium) or waterlogging. Signs: brown/black, slimy, foul-smelling roots. Plants wilt even when water is available.
Alkaline
Afrikaans: Alkalies		 pH above 7 Water with a pH above 7. In hydroponics, alkaline water usually means lime (calcium carbonate) is present in the water. Lower with pH-Down (phosphoric acid).
