The Three Core Measurements — What They Measure & Why They Differ
⚡
EC
Electrical Conductivity
Measures how well electricity passes through your water. More dissolved salts (nutrients) = higher conductivity. EC tells you the total strength of your nutrient solution — not which nutrients, just how much.
UnitmS/cm
Ideal NFT range1.0 – 2.2 mS/cm
Pure water EC≈ 0.0 mS/cm
💡 Think of EC as the "food concentration dial" — too low = hungry plants, too high = nutrient burn.
🧪
pH
Power of Hydrogen
Measures the concentration of hydrogen ions — i.e. how acidic or alkaline your water is. pH controls the chemical availability of nutrients. Even perfect EC is useless if pH locks out the nutrient the plant needs.
Scale0 (acid) → 14 (base)
Ideal NFT range5.5 – 6.8
Pure water pH7.0 (neutral)
💡 pH is the "key to the pantry" — the nutrients are there (EC is fine) but the wrong pH locks the door.
💧
TDS
Total Dissolved Solids
Measures the same thing as EC — total dissolved salts — but expressed in ppm (parts per million) instead of mS/cm. Most TDS meters simply measure conductivity and multiply by a conversion factor.
Unitppm (mg/L)
Conversion (×500)EC 1.5 → 750 ppm
Conversion (×700)EC 1.5 → 1050 ppm
💡 TDS and EC measure the same thing. Hydro growers use EC. Aquarium and drinking water use TDS. Know your meter's factor (×500 or ×700).

Too Little Oxygen — Causes
Root hypoxia → brown slime → plant death
Pump failure or power cut — stagnant water loses O₂ within 2–4 hours. Roots begin dying.
Water too warm (>26°C) — warm water holds less dissolved oxygen than cold water (Henry's Law).
Pipe slope too flat (<2°) — water pools instead of flowing, creating anaerobic dead zones.
Algae or bacteria bloom — these organisms consume O₂ faster than it can dissolve in, starving roots.
Root mass blocking pipe — over-grown roots block flow and eliminate the air gap in the pipe.
Timer set wrong — pump running 24/7 at low flow can cause pooling in some pipe layouts.
📈
Too Much Oxygen — Is It Possible?
In NFT, more O₂ is almost always better
In practice — not dangerous to roots. Unlike soil, there is no upper limit where excess O₂ harms root tissue in hydro systems.
Air pump too strong in tank — heavy bubbling makes EC readings inaccurate. Measure after bubbles settle.
Flow rate too aggressive — if water sprays rather than flows as a thin film, roots can dry out between cycles.
Signs of excellent O₂ — brilliant white roots, no smell from the tank, rapid plant growth, stable pH (no bacterial drift).
Rule of thumb: Keep water temperature under 22°C, maintain a 3–5° pipe slope, and run the pump at least 15 min every hour — oxygen takes care of itself.
